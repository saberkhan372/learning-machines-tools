# Optional Studio: Showcase
## What did we learn about generative AI by making with it?

**Learning Machines: Text, Images, Video — CC Fest**
**Duration:** 2 hours | **Format:** Virtual, Zoom

---

## What We'll Do

The optional studio is the showcase. There are no new concepts. Participants share what they built, found, or made across the three sessions. We close with a collective reflection — collected on the [Evidence Wall](https://saberkhan372.github.io/learning-machines-tools/tools/evidence-wall/) — and a short closing ritual.

By the end of this session participants will have:
- Presented their final project in 8–10 minutes
- Heard and responded to 4–6 other projects
- Named one thing they are taking back to their practice
- Identified one open question they are still holding

---

## Session Flow

| Time | Block | Format |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:10 | Welcome + framing | Facilitator-led |
| 0:10–1:25 | Presentations | Participants, ~10 min each |
| 1:25–1:50 | Collective reflection | Whole group |
| 1:50–2:00 | Closing | Facilitator-led |

---

## Opening (0:00–0:10)

> *[Scripted framing]*

Welcome to the optional studio. This is the session where the camp becomes yours.

Over the last three sessions we asked the same question in three different registers: what is the machine actually doing? We saw it predict tokens, draw defaults, drift across frames. We named what was human, what was machine, and what was assumed by the system.

Today you show what you found.

A few norms for the share-out:
- We respond to evidence, not to impressiveness.
- "That surprised me" and "I don't know what to make of that" are complete responses.
- We name what the human decided.
- We name what the model contributed and what human work, data, or labor made the output possible.

You have 8–10 minutes. There is no required format. Show what you made, explain what you found, or describe what you would teach. Then we ask two questions.

---

## Presentations (0:10–1:25)

### Format

Each participant presents for 8–10 minutes, followed by 2 minutes of response.

Facilitator keeps time. After each presentation, ask the group two questions:

1. **"What did you notice?"** — factual observation, not evaluation
2. **"What question does this open?"** — something the project raised that wasn't settled

Do not open the floor to general feedback. The two-question format keeps responses substantive and prevents the session from drifting into praise or critique.

After those two questions, run a 60-second credit scan before anything is archived or recapped:

1. **"What did the human do? What did the model do?"**
2. **"What should be credited, and to whom?"**
3. **"Whose work, data, or labor is hidden in this output?"**

Use the [AI Use + Consent Checklist](../worksheets/ai-use-consent-checklist/) for credit, consent, and recap decisions; do not create a new worksheet for this scan.

### What participants might bring (by pathway)

**Make**
A creative artifact — a text piece, image series, or video — with evidence of iteration. They should be able to show at least three versions and describe what the model did vs. what they decided.

**Teach**
A classroom-ready activity, worksheet, or facilitation plan. Ask them to run a 2-minute version of the activity with the group if possible.

**Investigate**
A model behavior experiment or Default Test. They should have a prompt, at least three examples or variations, and a written claim.

**Explain**
A tool, poster, zine, or concept bridge. Ask them to walk through the core idea.

**Critical / No-AI**
A critique, consent checklist, model card, policy draft, or unplugged activity. This pathway deserves the same time and attention as the others — it is not a consolation pathway.

### Facilitator notes for presentations

> - If a participant says "I didn't finish" or "I'm not sure this counts," respond: "Show us what you have. What you found in the process is the project."
> - Watch for participants who describe their project but never show it. Prompt: "Can you show us the actual output / artifact / document?"
> - If someone brought something from a different tool or context than the camp sessions, that is fine. The investigation protocol is transferable.
> - If a participant used no AI tools, their project is valid and often the most interesting in the debrief.

### Suggested presentation order

Consider ordering so that different pathways are distributed across the session — an investigator next to a maker next to a teacher. This keeps the energy varied and surfaces the range of what the investigation protocol can produce.

## Collective Reflection (1:25–1:50)

After all presentations, bring the group together for a structured collective reflection. This is facilitated, not open discussion.

### Round 1: One thing you are taking back (5 min)

Go around the group. Each person completes one sentence:

> *"I am taking back ___."*

No follow-up, no elaboration. Ask everyone to also type their sentence into Zoom chat, then paste the chat block into the [Evidence Wall](https://saberkhan372.github.io/learning-machines-tools/tools/evidence-wall/) — the round becomes a tiled wall on screen, attributed by name. The consent banner applies: mark tiles private before exporting or screenshotting anything for the recap.

### Round 2: One question you are still holding (5 min)

Same structure. Each person completes:

> *"I am still wondering ___."*

Collect these in chat and paste them into the same Evidence Wall. These questions become the shared open archive of the cohort — the wall's share link reproduces it for the recap (public tiles only).

### Round 3: What the machine can't do (10 min)

Open discussion — 10 minutes, no agenda. The prompt:

> *"Based on what you saw this summer: what is something the machine genuinely cannot do that you think matters?"*

Push for specificity: not "understand," but what understanding would require that prediction doesn't. Not "be creative," but what in the creative process was outside the model's contribution.

> *[Facilitator — you do not need to resolve this. The goal is to leave participants with a more precise vocabulary for talking about AI limits, not a settled answer.]*

---

## Closing (1:50–2:00)

> *[Scripted closing]*

What we did this summer was investigate. Not evaluate, not celebrate, not panic — investigate.

You built a protocol: hypothesis, controlled test, output comparison, evidence-based claim, ethical reflection. That protocol is portable. It works on any model, any modality, any tool that shows up in your school or your community or your studio.

The repeated question — what is the machine actually doing? — does not have a final answer. The systems change. The answers change. But the practice of asking, with evidence, with specificity, with attention to whose labor and whose images and whose stories shaped the training data — that practice is yours now.

A few things coming out of today:
- Session recordings and notes will be in the Notion workspace
- A cohort summary recap will go out to participants (reviewed before publishing)
- If your project is ready to share publicly, let us know how you want to be credited
- Tool updates will keep coming — your evidence shapes what gets refined or built next

Thank you for investigating with us.

## Facilitation Notes

**If a participant didn't complete a project:**
That is fine. Ask them to share something they observed, a question they are holding, or a moment from the camp that stayed with them. The showcase is not a grade.

**If presentations run long:**
Keep the two-question format strict. If the group is engaged in a rich exchange, let one presentation run to 15 minutes — but pull back time from collective reflection, not from other presentations.

**If presentations run short:**
Use the extra time in collective reflection. The "what the machine can't do" discussion often goes longer than expected and is usually the most substantive part of the session.

**After the camp:**
- Post the session recording and notes within 48 hours
- Send the cohort summary for review before publishing
- Log feedback on the tools in the Notion workspace
- Identify which second-wave tool would have been most useful for this cohort
