# Guest Speaker and Showcase Plan

---

## Guest Speaker Overview

Guest speakers appear in Optional Studio as an optional addition after participant presentations. The goal is not to add celebrity — it is to extend the investigation. A good guest speaker for this camp answers the same question participants spent three sessions on, from a different position:

> *"What is the machine actually doing — and what does that mean for your work?"*

---

## Invite Blurb

Use this or adapt it when reaching out to potential speakers:

---

*Hi [name],*

*I'm running Learning Machines: Text, Images, Video, a free creative AI camp for educators, artists, students, and curious learners. We spend three sessions investigating how language models, image models, and video models actually work — not how to use them better, but what they're doing mechanically and what they assume.*

*We're looking for a 15–20 minute guest slot in our optional studio showcase. The format is loose: you share something from your practice that connects to the camp's core question — "what is the machine actually doing?" — then take 5–10 minutes of questions from participants.*

*Participants are educators, artists, and researchers who have spent three sessions running controlled prompt experiments, analyzing model defaults, and investigating video failure modes. They're not looking for hype or warning. They're looking for someone who is thinking carefully about the same questions they are.*

*[If applicable: We're specifically interested in your work on [X] because it connects directly to what participants found in Session 2 / 3 / etc.]*

*No slides required. No preparation required beyond whatever you already know and care about. 20 minutes total.*

*[Your name and contact]*

---

## Speaker Archetypes

These are roles, not requirements. One strong speaker is better than five adequate ones.

### 1. The Critical AI Researcher

Someone who studies AI systems empirically — bias, fairness, capability evaluation, alignment. They can speak from evidence about what models do and what they don't do.

**What they offer:** Legitimacy for the investigation approach. Participants learn that professional researchers use the same empirical methods they just practiced.

**Good questions to ask them:**
- What is the most important thing a general audience gets wrong about how these models work?
- What would you want people to investigate that they usually don't?

---

### 2. The Artist or Designer Working with AI Critically

Someone whose practice involves AI tools, but who has a reflective relationship with them — not a booster, not a rejector, but an investigator.

**What they offer:** A model of how to make things with AI while maintaining critical judgment. Especially valuable for participants in the Make pathway.

**Good questions to ask them:**
- When did the model do something you kept versus something you had to revise or reject?
- What does the model not do that your creative process requires?

---

### 3. The Educator Who Teaches AI Literacy

A K–12 or higher ed educator who has developed classroom approaches to AI investigation. Especially useful if the camp audience is primarily educators.

**What they offer:** Concrete classroom applications. Participants in the Teach pathway see what it looks like to bring this work to students.

**Good questions to ask them:**
- What activity or investigation worked best with students?
- What got the most pushback — from students, parents, or administrators — and how did you handle it?

---

### 4. The Policy, Ethics, or Law Practitioner

Someone working on the regulatory, policy, or legal dimensions of generative AI — consent, copyright, labor, platform responsibility.

**What they offer:** The system-level view. Participants who spent three sessions looking at model defaults and failures now hear about the human and institutional structures around those systems.

**Good questions to ask them:**
- Where does the law currently fall short in addressing what participants observed in the camp?
- What is the one policy change you think would matter most?

---

### 5. The Data Worker, Annotator, or Content Moderator

Someone whose labor is in the training or operational pipeline — data labeling, content moderation, RLHF feedback. This archetype is the hardest to recruit and the most important.

**What they offer:** The labor perspective. Participants learn who makes the models work and under what conditions.

**Good questions to ask them:**
- What does the work actually feel like?
- What do the systems you work with not see about the people in them?

---

## Optional Studio Showcase Protocol

### Before the Session

- Confirm each participant has a project to share (or something to say about what they found)
- Confirm display names and consent for public sharing
- If a guest speaker is joining: brief them on the camp format and the investigation question; share 2–3 participant project summaries so they can connect their remarks

---

### Session Flow with Guest Speaker

| Time | Block |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:10 | Opening + norms |
| 0:10–1:10 | Participant presentations (~10 min each, 6 participants) |
| 1:10–1:35 | Guest speaker (15–20 min talk + 5 min Q&A) |
| 1:35–1:50 | Collective reflection (three rounds) |
| 1:50–2:00 | Closing |

*If no guest speaker: extend participant presentations or collective reflection.*

---

### Participant Presentation Protocol

**Format:** 8–10 minutes per participant, 2 minutes of group response.

**Response format (strict):**
After each presentation, two questions only:
1. *"What did you notice?"* — factual observation, not evaluation
2. *"What question does this open?"* — a genuine question the project raised

No praise. No suggestions. No "have you considered...?" These two questions keep responses substantive and prevent drift toward evaluation.

**Presentation order suggestion:**
Vary by pathway across the session: Make → Teach → Investigate → Explain → Critical → Make → etc. This keeps the energy varied and shows the full range of the investigation protocol.

---

### Guest Speaker Protocol

**Invite the speaker** after participant presentations, not before — so they are responding to what participants found, not setting the agenda.

**Introduction (30 seconds):** Name, role, and one sentence on why they are here.

**Talk (15–20 minutes):** Loose format. They share something from their practice or research that connects to the camp's question.

**Q&A (5 min):** Two or three participant questions. Facilitator moderates.

**Closing move:** Ask the speaker: *"Based on what you heard from participants today — what question would you want them to investigate next?"*

---

### Collective Reflection Protocol

**Three rounds, strictly timed:**

**Round 1 — Taking back (5 min)**
Go around. Each person completes: *"I am taking back ___."*
No elaboration. Facilitator records.

**Round 2 — Still wondering (5 min)**
Go around. Each person completes: *"I am still wondering ___."*
These questions go into the shared cohort archive.

**Round 3 — What the machine can't do (10 min)**
Open discussion. Prompt: *"Based on what you saw: what is something the machine genuinely cannot do that you think matters?"*
Push for specificity. You don't need to resolve it.

---

### After the Session

Within 48 hours:
- Post recording and notes to the Notion workspace
- Send a draft cohort summary to participants for review before publishing
- Collect consent confirmations from any participant whose work is being described publicly
- Log tool feedback and investigation findings in the Notion workspace
- Identify which second-wave tool would have been most useful for this cohort

Within one week:
- Publish the cohort summary (after human review)
- Follow up individually with participants who expressed interest in future programs
- Write a brief pilot retrospective: what worked, what didn't, what to change
