Session overview

The core question was: when a language model writes, what is it actually doing? We moved through five mechanisms — tokenization, prediction, temperature, attention, and alignment — not to exhaustively cover them but to make them visible enough to argue about.

The running theme was the gap between fluency and meaning. Every activity was designed to make that gap tangible: the model sounds certain because it outputs high-probability tokens, not because it knows. ELIZA at the end made this especially stark — it doesn't "know" anything, but it often felt warmer than expected.

Two patterns we named by the end:

  1. Fluency consistently outran accuracy in first impressions. The gap between "sounds right" and "is right" is the central risk in classroom use.
  2. ELIZA's responses felt warmer than expected even when the rule was fully visible. Fluency perception overrides mechanism knowledge — even when we can see exactly what's happening.

The prediction game

We started unplugged. Sentence stem: "The orange cat…" Everyone typed their next-word guess in Zoom chat simultaneously before anyone else's answer was visible.

The histogram tool I'd planned to use failed mid-demo. ("I totally messed that up, so we'll just do a manual count.") We tallied in chat instead — which worked better, because everyone could see the actual responses as they arrived.

Cha called it: walks had the most votes. The model's top prediction was also walks — the first word that follows "orange cat" more than any other in the training corpus. The room and the model weren't thinking the same thing; the room's distribution just happened to look like the model's distribution.

We then ran Count the Next Token to show the arithmetic: count how many times each word follows the stem in the corpus, divide by the total, get a probability. That's the whole mechanism — no understanding, just a very large fraction.

Teaching note

The unplugged prediction game is fully portable — sentence stems on a slide, chat or hands-raised for guesses, whiteboard tally. No logins, no AI use, no accounts. Margot's guess ("bookshop") was the most interesting pedagogically: it was far from the modal response but made complete grammatical and narrative sense. That's a useful discussion move — why did Margot guess differently, and does that difference matter to the model?

Tokenizer + Temperature

We opened the Tokenizer + Temperature Visualizer and looked at two things in sequence: how the tokenizer splits text (often not where a human reader would cut), then how temperature slides output from predictable to surprising.

The key demonstration was the same prompt at low temperature vs. high temperature — low was safe and repetitive, high was surprising and sometimes unstable. The dial isn't adding noise arbitrarily; it's deliberately widening the distribution that the model samples from. Low temperature collapses the distribution to its mode; high temperature flattens it.

Angela noticed that even "surprising" outputs at high temperature still felt coherent at a surface level — they were grammatically fine, just semantically strange. That observation set up the ELIZA comparison well: fluency is a separate property from accuracy.

— Angela, during the temperature demo

The trophy-case flip (attention)

To make attention visible, we used a single sentence with one-word change:

  • "The trophy didn't fit in the case because it was too big" → it refers to the trophy
  • "The trophy didn't fit in the case because it was too small" → it refers to the case

One word — big vs. small — flips which object the pronoun refers to across the whole sentence. The model has to hold the entire sentence in relation to itself to resolve it correctly. That's attention: routing reference across distance.

Meghan and Randy each appear in both rows — they caught the flip when the sentence changed. That's exactly the exercise: notice how your own interpretation shifts with one word, then understand that the model does something structurally similar, across the whole context, for every token it generates.

The homework apology (alignment / RLHF)

We showed two homework apology texts — Team A (specific accountability, new timeline, acknowledgment of impact) and Team B (vague, no concrete plan) — and asked which one was better and why.

The room almost universally picked Team A. That's the point: there's no "correct" answer the model discovers from text alone. Knowing that A is better took human judgment — RLHF teams rating responses, scoring what "better" means, feeding that signal back into training. The fluent model inherited that judgment.

"B implies the work has already been potentially turned in — by saying 'I thought it was fine as is,' the student is saying I already submitted it and I'm not going to fix it."

— Shane, on why he picked Team A

"More details and specific tokens."

— Meghan, on what made Team A better

"Owning responsibility."

— Judy

Judy also mentioned a South Park episode about AI — a useful cultural touchstone for the "it sounds like a person" anxiety. The apology exercise works precisely because it shows the moment where "sounding like a person" is real but the source of that quality is human labor, not machine understanding.

ELIZA vs. LLM

We closed the mechanism tour with ELIZA — the 1966 rule-based chatbot that matches patterns and substitutes pronouns. The exercise: inspect the actual rule ELIZA matched, see the template it selected, read the output. Completely transparent. Then compare to a pre-generated LLM response to the same input.

ELIZA consistently felt warmer than it should. Even after we showed the rule — I (.*) you → Perhaps in your fantasies we \1 each other — the outputs still read as somewhat sympathetic. That's the stability of the illusion: knowing the mechanism doesn't dissolve the perception.

Yunseo gave the clearest summary of the difference: ELIZA uses fixed rule-based weights (explicit pattern → template mappings), while an LLM uses learned weights that are unfixed — shaped by training but not readable as rules. The architecture is categorically different, but the surface experience can feel similar.

— Yunseo

"i loveee these questions / i feel like this is really where the crux of the learning happens"

— Angela, during the ELIZA / LLM architecture discussion

Angela pushed on whether ELIZA's "rule-based" label was precise — is the pattern matching closer to a lookup table or something else? The answer is: it's a sequence of regex-like patterns, evaluated in order, and the first match wins. Nothing is learned. That contrast with gradient descent is sharp and teachable.

Ryan shared the original ELIZA implementation from masswerk.at — the closest thing to the 1966 Weizenbaum program still running.

Local LLMs tangent

Shane opened a thread about running models locally. He'd been experimenting with Ollama — running a frontier model locally and watching it interact with a smaller model.

"If you use a frontier model to help you set up a local LLM, it's fun, watching it get frustrated talking to a stupider machine is wild."

— Shane, on Ollama + local model setup

"Bonzai lol"

— Shane, later in the same thread

Shane also connected local LLMs to TinyML and embedded ML. The distinction between a frontier cloud model and something running on a microcontroller is enormous, but both involve the same core loop: data in, pattern out. The scale difference is pedagogically useful.

Ryan shared several tools in this thread: Ollama for local model serving, Google Edge Gallery for on-device ML, and micro:bit AI for embedded educational contexts.

Guest spotlight

Dr. Tiffany Tseng

Design Tools Lab, Barnard College · research on creative tools and human-computer interaction

Tiffany joined for the closing discussion and landed the question that reframed the whole session: "How does AI change the work?" rather than "Does it replace creativity?" The shift matters — the first question is empirical and observable, the second tends toward abstraction.

Her lab's ml-keyframer project explores exactly this: how does an AI-assisted keyframing tool change what animators actually do, not just whether they "still" do it? The answer from that research is nuanced — the tool changes the kind of decisions that remain in the human's hands, not the quantity of decisions.

Shane connected this immediately to vibe coding: you let go of the mental model because the output looks stable. That's the same dynamic Tiffany's research surfaces — the risk isn't replacement, it's that the visible output quality decouples from the depth of understanding behind it.

Teaching notes

Backup for the histogram tool

The live histogram failed. The Zoom chat tally worked better anyway — participants could see each other's guesses arriving in real time, which added social dynamics the histogram missed. The low-tech version (sentence stem on a slide, guesses in chat, manual tally on a whiteboard or in a doc) is fully sufficient and probably preferable for the debrief.

The unplugged route works for almost everything

The prediction game, the trophy-case exercise, and the homework apology comparison all work with zero AI use. ELIZA needs a browser but no accounts. Only the Temperature Visualizer requires live model inference. A fully no-AI version of this session is viable — see the No-AI participation guide for the detailed pathway.

The ML vs. LLM framing matters

The distinction raised at the start (with the self-driving car analogy) prevents a common conflation: treating LLMs as "AI" as a category rather than one architecture among many. A perspective beyond LLMs made that distinction land differently than it would have from a slide.

Angela's point on high-temperature fluency

She noticed that even strange high-temperature outputs were grammatically coherent. This is worth foregrounding in future sessions: fluency is a property of the output format, not of the content. A model can produce well-formed sentences that say nothing. That's true at any temperature.
