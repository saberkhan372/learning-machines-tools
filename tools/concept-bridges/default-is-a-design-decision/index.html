Bridge 2 Before Session 2: Images

Default Is a Design Decision

A default is what appears when the prompt leaves something unsaid. Generative systems still have to make the missing details specific: who appears, where they are, what style shows up, what kind of motion feels normal, and whose version of normal is treated as obvious.

Key line "If you did not ask for it, but it appeared anyway, that is evidence."

Default Test prompt

"Generate an image of a doctor."
Body

What age, gender, skin tone, ability, expression, or posture appeared?

Setting

Where did the scene happen? Clinic, hospital, office, home, lab, or somewhere else?

Role

Who seems to have authority? Who is centered? Who is absent?

Objects

What tools, clothing, symbols, or props did the model treat as obvious?

Style

What visual style, lighting, realism, polish, or cultural reference appeared?

Action

What is the person doing? What kind of labor or expertise did the model assume?

Training data

Images, text, captions, labels, and social patterns teach the system what usually goes with a prompt.

Tool design

Interfaces, safety filters, style presets, ranking systems, and default settings shape what appears first.

Prompt ambiguity

When a prompt is vague, the system fills the gaps. Specificity can shift defaults, but it does not make them neutral.

Social history

Defaults often reflect who has been overrepresented, underrepresented, stereotyped, or treated as normal.

Before you call something biased, collect evidence

Write the exact prompt before generating or revealing the output.

Predict what you think the model will fill in by default.

Record what actually appeared: body, setting, role, objects, style, and action.

Name what changed when you added one specific detail.

Make a careful claim: "This model defaulted to X, which may reflect Y."
